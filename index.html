What is Carbon Capture and Storage?

CCS is a suite of technologies designed to prevent large amounts of carbon dioxide (CO₂) from being released into the atmosphere. Positioned at the intersection of industrial necessity and technological challenge, CCS is increasingly recognized as an indispensable tool for decarbonizing sectors where emissions are otherwise unavoidable.

Executive Summary

The strategic importance of CCS is underscored by its central role in climate mitigation scenarios of the IEA and IPCC. It's considered virtually the only viable pathway for deep emissions reductions in heavy industries such as cement, steel, and chemicals.

As of early 2025, over 50 million tonnes of annual capture capacity are operational, with the pipeline promising a near nine-fold increase to approximately 430 Mt per year by 2030, driven primarily by policy support like the U.S. Section 45Q tax credit.

1

Capture

CO₂ is separated from other gases produced by industrial processes, like those at power plants or steel factories, using advanced chemical and physical separation techniques.

2

Transport

The captured CO₂ is compressed into a supercritical state and transported via dedicated pipelines or specialized ships to carefully selected storage sites.

3

Storage

The CO₂ is injected deep underground into carefully selected geological formations for permanent storage, secured by multiple trapping mechanisms.

Capture & Storage Techniques

The methods for capturing and storing CO₂ vary based on the industrial source and local geology. The capture process is the most complex and costly part of CCS. Explore the primary techniques used today.

Post-Combustion Capture

This is the most mature and widely deployed capture methodology, where CO₂ is captured from flue gases after the fuel has been burned in a conventional combustion process using air. The process typically uses a chemical solvent to absorb the CO₂, which is then heated to release the pure CO₂ for compression and transport.

Key Technical Details:

CO₂ concentration: 3-4% (natural gas) to 13-15% (coal)
Capture rates: Typically over 90%, with rates exceeding 95%
Technology: Amine-based solvents in absorber/stripper columns
Energy penalty: Can increase electricity costs by up to 66%

Main Advantage:

Retrofitting capability - it can be added to existing power plants and industrial facilities without major modifications to the original combustion process, providing a crucial pathway to decarbonize long-lived assets.

Pre-Combustion Capture

In this method, carbon is removed from the fuel source before it is burned. The primary fuel undergoes gasification or steam methane reforming, reacting with steam and oxygen to form synthesis gas (syngas) - primarily carbon monoxide and hydrogen.

Process Flow:

  1. Fuel undergoes gasification/steam reforming
  2. Water-gas-shift reaction converts CO to CO₂ + H₂
  3. High-pressure CO₂ separation (15-50% concentration)
  4. Hydrogen-rich fuel used for power generation

Advantages:

  • Higher thermodynamic efficiency for capture
  • Uses physical solvents (less energy-intensive)
  • Core technology for low-carbon "blue" hydrogen production
  • Lower cost increase (~37% vs 66% for post-combustion)

This is more complex and typically better suited for new-build facilities rather than retrofits. It's fundamentally integrated into facility design and represents the pathway for hydrogen economy development.

Oxy-Fuel Combustion

This technique involves burning fuel in nearly pure oxygen instead of regular air, producing a flue gas that is mostly water vapor and CO₂. This redesigned combustion environment facilitates easy carbon capture.

Process Requirements:

  • Cryogenic Air Separation Unit (ASU)
  • Combustion in ~pure oxygen environment
  • Flue gas recycling for temperature control
  • Simple CO₂/water separation by cooling

Key Benefits:

  • Near 100% capture rates possible
  • High-purity CO₂ stream (~90% dry basis)
  • 60-70% reduction in NOx formation
  • Simplified CO₂ processing

Main Challenge:

The ASU is energy-intensive and represents significant capital and operational costs. However, the technology has been demonstrated at pilot scale, such as at the Schwarze Pumpe facility in Germany.

Can be applied to both new-build plants and, with significant modifications, to existing facilities. Represents the highest capture efficiency option available today.

Direct Air Capture (DAC)

DAC functions like an industrial-scale "air filter" for the planet, extracting CO₂ directly from ambient atmosphere at ~420 ppm (0.04%). Unlike point source methods, DAC plants can be built anywhere, addressing emissions from dispersed sources and legacy emissions.

Liquid DAC (L-DAC):

  • Uses aqueous potassium hydroxide (KOH)
  • High temperature regeneration (300-900°C)
  • Forms carbonate salts
  • Higher energy requirements

Solid DAC (S-DAC):

  • Uses solid sorbent materials
  • Lower temperature regeneration (80-120°C)
  • CO₂ adsorbs to surface
  • More energy efficient

Current Status & Economics:

As of early 2024: 27 commissioned facilities globally (~0.01 Mt/year), 130+ planned facilities

Cost range: $152-$600+ per tonne CO₂ (target: <$100/tonne for gigaton-scale)

DAC provides the "net" in net-zero, addressing impossible-to-eliminate emissions. Revenue comes from carbon removal credits or high-value products like synthetic aviation fuels.

Permanent Storage Methods

Once captured, CO₂ must be stored securely for centuries. Storage sites require two key features: porous reservoir rock for injection and impermeable caprock to prevent migration. Four trapping mechanisms ensure long-term security.

Primary Storage Formations:

Depleted Oil & Gas Reservoirs
  • Well-understood geology from decades of production
  • Proven containment for millions of years
  • Existing infrastructure can be repurposed
  • Limited by original hydrocarbon volume
Deep Saline Aquifers
  • Largest potential storage resource globally
  • Filled with non-potable saline water
  • Geographically widespread
  • Require significant geological assessment
Basalt Formations
  • Reactive mineralogy enables rapid carbonation
  • CO₂ converts to solid carbonate quickly
  • Enhanced permanence and security
  • Limited geographical availability

Trapping Mechanisms (Increasing Security Over Time):

1. Structural Trapping

Immediate: CO₂ rises until trapped beneath impermeable caprock

2. Residual Trapping

Short-term: CO₂ droplets trapped in pore spaces by capillary forces

3. Solubility Trapping

Decades: CO₂ dissolves in brine, becomes denser and sinks

4. Mineral Trapping

Millennia: CO₂ reacts with rock to form permanent carbonate minerals

Storage Security & Monitoring:

IPCC projects well-managed sites will retain >99% of stored CO₂ for >1,000 years. All projects require comprehensive Measurement, Monitoring, and Verification (MMV) programs using:

  • Seismic imaging to track CO₂ plume movement
  • Well-based monitoring of reservoir pressure
  • Caprock and well integrity verification
  • Surface and groundwater monitoring

Commerciality Gap

CCS requires carbon prices of $25-30/tonne to begin deployment, but EU ETS historically too low. Government subsidies bridge this gap.

Industrial Competitiveness

CCS increases steel costs <10%, while green hydrogen routes increase costs 35-70%. Often the least-cost decarbonization path.

Stranded Assets

Retrofitting with CCS avoids premature retirement of trillions in industrial assets, maintaining operational life in low-carbon world.

Hub Models

Shared transport & storage infrastructure spreads costs across multiple users, reducing per-ton costs and barriers to entry.

Job Creation

U.S. Direct Air Capture industry alone projected to create 300,000+ jobs in construction, engineering, and manufacturing at scale.

Investment Scale

Meeting climate targets requires 70-fold capacity increase by 2040, demanding trillions in investment and stable policy frameworks.

Challenges & Risk Management

While technically mature, CCS faces significant deployment challenges beyond technology, including operational risks, long-term liability, and public acceptance.

⚠️

Technical Risks

  • • Storage security & potential leakage pathways
  • • Infrastructure corrosion from CO₂ + water
  • • Induced seismicity from pressure changes
  • • High energy penalty (reduces plant efficiency)
⚖️

Legal & Liability

  • • Long-term liability (centuries-scale storage)
  • • Pore space ownership uncertainties
  • • Complex permitting processes
  • • Financial assurance requirements
👥

Social License

  • • Low public awareness & understanding
  • • NIMBY resistance to local projects
  • • "False solution" criticism from some groups
  • • Trust in operators & regulators
💰

Economic Challenges

  • • "Subsidy cliff" when incentives expire
  • • High upfront capital requirements
  • • Slow cost reduction vs. renewables
  • • Project execution risk ("valley of death")
🔧

Operational Issues

  • • Operating costs growing faster than capacity
  • • Underperformance vs. design targets
  • • Complex integration with existing plants
  • • Long-term monitoring requirements
🏗️

Infrastructure Scale

  • • Pipeline network needs 100x expansion
  • • Complex permit acquisition for corridors
  • • Coordination across multiple stakeholders
  • • Regional hub development challenges

Risk Mitigation Strategies

Technical Solutions:

  • Rigorous site characterization & selection
  • Advanced monitoring systems (MMV)
  • Material selection for corrosion resistance
  • Pressure management protocols

Policy & Social:

  • Early community engagement programs

  • Long-term policy certainty

Geological & Seismic Impacts

Large-scale CO₂ injection impacts subsurface geology through geomechanical and geochemical processes. Understanding these interactions ensures safe, permanent storage.

Geomechanical Effects

Induced Seismicity Risk

CO₂ injection changes subsurface pressure, potentially affecting pre-existing faults:

Risk Factors:
  • • Proximity to critically-stressed faults
  • • Injection rate and pressure magnitude
  • • Pre-existing stress conditions
  • • Geological structure complexity
Reality Check:
  • • Most events are micro-seismic (not felt)
  • • No large earthquakes from CCS to date
  • • Risk is site-specific and manageable

Surface Deformation

Reservoir pressure changes affect rock stress and surface elevation:

Depletion Effects:
  • • Oil/gas extraction causes rock compaction
  • • Surface subsidence up to 24cm possible
  • • Well-documented in many fields globally
Re-injection Effects:
  • • CO₂ injection re-pressurizes reservoir
  • • Can cause slight surface uplift
  • • Monitored using satellite InSAR technology

Geochemical Reactions

CO₂-Rock-Fluid Interactions

1. Acidification

CO₂ dissolves in brine → carbonic acid → lower pH environment

2. Mineral Dissolution

Acidic brine dissolves carbonates & feldspars → increased porosity

3. Precipitation

New minerals form (siderite, clays) → potential permeability changes

Operational Implications:

Positive:

  • Enhanced storage capacity from dissolution
  • Long-term mineral trapping potential

Challenges:

  • Pore throat clogging from precipitation
  • Reduced injectivity over time

Monitoring Technologies:

Scientific Consensus:

While CO₂ injection can induce seismicity and cause geochemical changes, these risks are well-understood and manageable through proper site selection, pressure management, and comprehensive monitoring. The IPCC concludes with "high confidence" that appropriately selected, designed, and managed storage sites can permanently isolate CO₂ from the atmosphere.
